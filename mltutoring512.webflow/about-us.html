How does Machine Learning work?

The gold standard for whether or not Machine Learning is working is if a computer can classify as well as a human. This means that as we work through our deforestation example, we must evaluate whether or not the machine is seeing and classifying our images as well as a human eye and brain can. This is important, because if a human can’t do classification, how could we expect a computer to be able to? 
Two major stages in doing Machine Learning is training and testing with our picture data. In the training stage, we will decide the best rules for separating our picture data using labeled examples in a subset of our picture dataset. Essentially, we train our computer using examples that we’ve already labeled. In the testing stage, we will see how well our rules work when they are applied to the new examples that make up the rest of our picture dataset.

Where did we get the data?

Machine Learning requires data. To answer our question about deforestation, we will be using pictures as our data. Our picture data comes from Google Earth. We took screenshots of satellite imagery from Indonesia from the year 2000. Then, we took screenshots of the exact same areas from the year 2015. This way, we were able to capture change over time.
